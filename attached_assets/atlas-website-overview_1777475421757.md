# Atlas

A personal command center for the way you actually work.

---

## What Atlas is

Atlas is one place for the things that make up your day — your tasks, your calendar, the people you're in touch with, your notes, your journal. Most productivity tools are good at one of these and force you to bolt on others. Atlas brings them together so the connections between them — the meeting you have tomorrow, the person you're meeting, the project you'll discuss, the notes you took last week — actually live in one system.

It's built for people who think carefully about how they work. If you've tried OmniFocus and Notion and Things and a calendar app and a journaling app and felt the friction of moving between them, Atlas is for you.

This is currently in private use among family and friends. It's not a commercial product, just something carefully made.

---

## The five modules

### Tasks

Your inbox for everything that needs to happen. Capture a thought in two seconds — type it, speak it, email it to your Atlas inbox — and it lives somewhere you'll actually see it again. Organize tasks into projects when you're ready, or leave them in the inbox until you have time to think about them.

The system gets out of your way when you're capturing and helps you focus when you're working. You can see what's due today, what's flagged as important, what's coming up this week, what's stalled and needs review. The bar for capture is low (capture anything, anytime, anywhere) and the bar for focus is high (the right things in front of you at the right time).

If you've used GTD-style tools before, you'll find what you expect: contexts, projects, deferred dates, a weekly review, hierarchical task organization. If you haven't, you'll find a system that's still simple enough to use without learning a methodology.

### Calendar

Your time, visible in one place. Atlas connects to your existing Google Calendar and shows your meetings alongside your tasks — so when you're planning your week, you see both what you've committed to and what you want to get done.

You can see whether tomorrow has space for that deep-work block you've been meaning to do, or whether it's already full of meetings. Over time, Atlas will help you actively schedule tasks into the gaps in your calendar — not as a rigid system, but as a way to be honest with yourself about what fits in the time you have.

### People

The contacts and conversations that matter, kept warm. Atlas connects to your Google Contacts and helps you remember the people in your life — not as data records, but as ongoing relationships. When you're capturing a task that mentions someone ("call Ahmed about Q2"), Atlas links it. When you open a person, you see what's been on your mind about them, what conversations you've had, what's coming up.

This isn't a CRM in the corporate sense. It's a tool for staying genuinely connected with the people you care about — family, close friends, colleagues whose relationships matter beyond transactions.

### Notes

The thinking you want to keep. Project briefs, meeting notes, reading notes, ideas, half-formed thoughts that might mature into something. Atlas's notes are markdown documents — clean, simple, fast — but with one important difference: they connect.

A note about a project links to the project's tasks. A note from a meeting links to the people who were there and the calendar event. A reference to an idea you wrote about last month links to that earlier note. Over time, the system you build inside Atlas becomes a network of your own thinking, not a flat list of files.

### Journals

The ongoing record of your days. A daily journal is for processing — what happened, what you learned, what's on your mind, what you're noticing. Atlas's journal is private, simple, and lives alongside everything else: when you reference a project in your journal entry, you can come back to that project later and see the journal entries that mentioned it.

This isn't required. Many people use Atlas without journaling. But if you're someone who finds value in writing about your days — for clarity, for memory, for working through what's on your mind — Atlas gives you somewhere to do it that connects to the rest of your life.

## How the modules work together

The point of Atlas isn't five tools in one app. It's that the boundaries between them disappear when you actually use them.

A meeting on Tuesday afternoon is a calendar event, but Atlas knows the project it's about and the person you're meeting. The notes you take during the meeting connect back to both. The follow-up tasks you capture afterward link to the project, the person, and the meeting itself. A month later, when you're preparing for a similar conversation, all of it is one click away.

When you reference a person, project, idea, or earlier thought, Atlas recognizes it and creates the connection. You don't have to think about organizing — the structure builds itself from how you actually work.

---

## What Atlas is not

It's not a team collaboration tool. There's no shared workspace, no commenting, no real-time editing with others. Atlas is for one person managing their own life and work.

It's not a replacement for specialized tools. If you need a sophisticated project management system for a software team, use Linear or Jira. If you need a beautiful personal wiki, use Obsidian. If you need email, use email. Atlas is for the *integrative* layer — the personal command center that ties together everything you actually do across whatever specialized tools you use.

It's not a habit tracker, a focus timer, an analytics dashboard, or a quantified self tool. It's a system for thinking about and organizing your life, not for measuring it.

## How to get started

If you've been invited to Atlas, you'll receive an invitation by email. Sign in with Google or use the magic link. You'll land in your empty Tasks module — capture something to begin.

There's no tutorial. The interface is meant to feel familiar from the first minute. If something is unclear, ask whoever invited you — that's how a tool built for the people you know is supposed to work.

---

## A note on privacy and data

Atlas is hosted privately. Your data — your tasks, your notes, your journal entries — lives in databases owned and controlled by the person who invited you, not on shared servers analyzed for advertising.

Atlas uses AI to help with capture parsing — when you write "call Ahmed about Q2 partnership next Tuesday," it figures out the structure. Most captures are handled locally without any external AI call; only ambiguous ones are sent to a language model for parsing. The content is used to parse your input, not to train models, and isn't sold or analyzed for advertising.

If you'd rather not have any AI involvement in your capture flow, you can turn it off in settings. Atlas works fine without it; you just lose the smart parsing of natural language.
